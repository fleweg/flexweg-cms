French bulldogs are one of the most popular breeds in the world right now, and that popularity has come with a real health cost. Knowing what to watch for makes the difference between catching something at week one and catching it at week six.

## 1. Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome (BOAS)

The flat face that makes frenchies frenchies also gives them a shortened upper airway. Most dogs in the breed have some degree of BOAS. Signs to watch for: snoring that's getting louder, exercise intolerance, blue-tinged gums after exertion, regurgitating right after eating.

If your dog has more than mild snoring or struggles in moderate weather, ask your vet about a BOAS grading assessment. Surgical correction exists and is much more effective at age 1–2 than at age 6.

## 2. Intervertebral Disc Disease (IVDD)

Their long backs and short legs put pressure on the spine. About 30% of frenchies will develop IVDD in their lifetime. Warning signs are unwillingness to jump, a hunched posture, dragging back paws, or sudden whimpering when picked up.

Prevention: don't let them jump off furniture (ramps help), keep them at a healthy weight, harness instead of collar.

## 3. Skin and ear allergies

Probably the most common issue you'll deal with. Allergies show up as itchy paws, repeated ear infections, and chronic skin redness. The cause is usually environmental (pollen, dust mites) more often than food, but food trials are worth doing.

## 4. Hip dysplasia

Less common than in larger breeds but still relevant. Watch for stiffness after rest, reluctance to climb stairs, or a "bunny hop" gait when running. X-rays are diagnostic.

## 5. Cherry eye

A protruding gland in the corner of the eye, usually red and visible. Surgical replacement (not removal — keep that tear gland working) is the standard fix.

## When to call the vet

Anything sudden — sudden lameness, sudden breathing difficulty, sudden lethargy — is an emergency. For chronic stuff, schedule earlier rather than later. Frenchies hide pain well.
