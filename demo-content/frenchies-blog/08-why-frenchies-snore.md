Anyone who's lived with a French bulldog knows the soundtrack. The snorting, the heavy breathing, the surprisingly loud naps. Most of it is just how they're built. Some of it warrants attention.

## What causes the snoring

The same anatomy that gives frenchies their distinctive face also gives them their distinctive sounds. The most common contributors:

**Elongated soft palate.** The tissue at the back of the roof of the mouth is longer than the jaw can accommodate. It vibrates when air passes over it during sleep.

**Stenotic nares.** Narrow nostrils. Air has to push through smaller openings, which produces noise.

**Everted laryngeal saccules.** Small sacs near the vocal cords that, in some frenchies, get sucked into the airway and add to the obstruction.

These features together are called BOAS — Brachycephalic Obstructive Airway Syndrome. Almost every French bulldog has at least one, often all three, in varying degrees.

## What's normal

A snoring frenchie who sleeps well, recovers from exercise within a few minutes, and has pink gums after walks is probably fine. The noise is uncomfortable to listen to (especially in shared bedrooms), but it's part of the package.

## What's not normal

Talk to your vet if you see:

- **Sleep apnea.** Long pauses (5+ seconds) between breaths while sleeping, followed by a sudden gasp. This is different from snoring — it's an active obstruction.
- **Cyanotic gums.** Blue or purple gums during or after sleep is a low-oxygen sign and needs immediate evaluation.
- **Worsening over time.** Snoring that's getting louder, more frequent, or extending into waking hours.
- **Coughing fits, regurgitation after meals, or visible breathing struggle during normal activity.**

## What can be done

For mild BOAS, the answer is often management: keep the dog lean, avoid heat, don't push exercise. Excess weight makes airway obstruction worse — even half a pound matters on a small dog.

For moderate or severe BOAS, surgery is the standard intervention. A board-certified veterinary surgeon can shorten the soft palate, widen the nostrils, and remove inflamed laryngeal tissue. The earlier this is done (ideally before age 2), the better the long-term outcome. Many frenchies who'd otherwise develop secondary problems — collapsed trachea, chronic regurgitation, exercise intolerance — never get there if the airway is corrected young.

If you have a young frenchie and you've never had a BOAS assessment, it's worth asking. Some breed-specialist clinics offer dedicated functional grading exams that give a clearer picture than a standard wellness visit.

## A note on sleeping arrangements

If the snoring is interfering with your sleep, a separate bed (theirs, not yours) in another room is fair to everyone. Frenchies adapt fast to sleeping locations. Worth doing at the puppy stage.
