Most home cooks chop with the same panicked grip they had at age twelve. There's a better way, and learning it pays off forever — you'll spend less time on prep, get more consistent cuts (which means food that cooks evenly), and stop cutting yourself.

You don't need fancy knives. One sharp chef's knife — 20 cm / 8 inch — will handle 90% of home cooking. Sharp is the operative word. A dull knife is the most dangerous tool in your kitchen.

## The grip

**Pinch grip on the knife.** Thumb and forefinger pinch the blade just above the handle. The other three fingers wrap the handle. This gives you control over the blade tip, not just the handle.

**Claw on the food hand.** Curl your fingertips back so your knuckles are forward. The flat of the blade rests against your knuckles as a guide. You can't cut your fingertips if they're tucked back.

That's it. Spend a week consciously using these two grips and they become automatic.

## 1. Rocking chop

For herbs, garlic, and small things. Tip of the blade stays on the board; you rock the heel of the knife up and down through the food. Move the food under the knife with your other hand. Faster, more controlled, and less hand fatigue than the chop-chop-chop most people start with.

## 2. Slicing

For tomatoes, mushrooms, cucumber. Long forward strokes, drawing the blade toward you. Let the knife do the work — pressing down only crushes soft food. A serrated edge helps with tomatoes if your chef's knife isn't very sharp.

## 3. Dice

The four-step dice for an onion: peel, halve through the root, slice horizontally and vertically (keeping the root end intact), then cross-cut to dice. The root holds everything together so the cuts come out uniform. It looks complicated written down. It takes 90 seconds once you've done it five times.

## 4. Brunoise

A 2–3 mm cube. Start with a stick (julienne), then turn 90° and slice across. Used for carrots, celery, onion in mirepoix; for elegant garnishes; for vegetables you want cooked through in 3 minutes. The skill carries over to almost everything.

## 5. Chiffonade

For leafy herbs and greens (basil, mint, kale, spinach). Stack the leaves, roll them tight like a cigar, slice across thinly. You get ribbons. Faster than chopping; doesn't bruise the herbs as much (which matters for basil especially — bruised basil turns black).

## Two pieces of equipment

**A sharpening steel.** Use it every time you cook for more than 20 minutes. It doesn't sharpen — it realigns the edge. A sharp knife stays sharp twice as long if you steel it regularly.

**A good cutting board.** End-grain wood is best (gentler on the blade). Plastic is fine. Glass is terrible — it dulls knives immediately. Wash your wooden board with soap and water, dry it standing up, oil it once a month with food-grade mineral oil.

Practice every time you cook. Five months from now you'll wonder how you ever did it differently.
