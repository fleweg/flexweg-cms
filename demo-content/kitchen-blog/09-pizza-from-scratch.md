Most home pizzas are sad because the dough is sad. Pre-made dough from the supermarket is too soft, makes too much, and tastes faintly of cardboard. Two-day dough from scratch — flour, water, salt, yeast, fridge — is the difference between "yeah, it's pizza I guess" and "this is genuinely good."

## The dough

Two-day cold fermentation. Day one is 10 minutes of work; day two is 30 minutes of cooking.

### Ingredients (makes 4 small pizzas)

- 500 g strong bread flour or 00 flour (Italian fine-milled — worth seeking out)
- 325 ml water (lukewarm, not hot)
- 10 g salt
- 2 g instant yeast (about half a teaspoon)
- Olive oil

### Day 1 (10 minutes of active work)

**1. Mix.** In a large bowl: flour, salt, yeast (keep salt and yeast on opposite sides of the bowl when adding — direct contact kills the yeast). Add water. Mix with a spatula until no dry flour remains. The dough will be very shaggy.

**2. Rest 30 minutes.** Cover the bowl with a tea towel.

**3. Stretch and fold.** Wet your hand. Reach under one side of the dough, stretch it up, fold it over the centre. Rotate the bowl 90°. Repeat three more times. The dough becomes smoother. Total time: about 2 minutes.

**4. Wait another 30 minutes. Repeat the stretch and fold.** Twice total.

**5. Refrigerate.** Lightly oil a clean bowl, place the dough in it, cover with cling film. Into the fridge. Minimum 24 hours, ideally 48–72.

The fridge does the work. The yeast ferments slowly, builds flavor, develops the gluten structure. This is where the magic is.

### Day 2

**1. Take the dough out of the fridge 2 hours before you want to eat.**

**2. Divide.** Cut into 4 equal pieces. Shape each into a tight ball. Place on a floured surface, cover with a tea towel. Rest 90 minutes.

**3. Heat the oven as hot as it goes.** 250°C / 480°F minimum, 280°C / 540°F if your oven goes that high. A pizza stone or steel inside is a massive upgrade — preheat it for at least 45 minutes. A heavy cast-iron pan flipped upside down is a passable substitute.

**4. Shape the pizzas.** On a well-floured surface, press each ball into a rough circle with your fingertips, leaving a slightly thicker rim. Pick up and stretch by hand — don't use a rolling pin (it crushes the bubbles you just built).

**5. Top.** Less is more. A thin layer of crushed San Marzano tomatoes (just tomatoes, salt, a pinch of sugar — no need to cook them first). A few torn pieces of fresh mozzarella. A few basil leaves. A drizzle of olive oil. Don't overload.

**6. Bake.** 6–8 minutes per pizza, watching closely. The crust should be charred in spots, the cheese melted, the bottom dark golden.

## Topping ideas

- **Margherita:** tomato, mozzarella, basil, oil
- **Bianca:** no tomato — olive oil, garlic, mozzarella, parmesan, fresh thyme
- **Funghi:** tomato base, mozzarella, sautéed mushrooms (cooked first or they release water onto the pizza), thyme
- **Diavola:** tomato, mozzarella, spicy salami, chili flakes

## Things that go wrong (and fixes)

- **The dough is sticky and won't stretch.** Either it didn't have enough time (try another hour at room temperature) or you're working with too little flour underneath. Generous floured surface.
- **The bottom is pale.** Oven not hot enough, or the dough was sitting on a soggy topping too long. Get the heat up, top sparingly.
- **The crust is dense and bread-like.** The dough overproofed (too warm, too long). Next time: shorter rest, cooler kitchen.

## The verdict

Once you've made pizza this way, supermarket dough loses all appeal. Two days of fridge time becomes part of the weekend rhythm — mix on Friday evening, bake on Sunday afternoon. Three minutes of work spread across two days produces something you couldn't buy in your neighborhood unless your neighborhood is Naples.

Worth it.
