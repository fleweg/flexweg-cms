Sourdough starter is alive. That sentence sounds dramatic, but it's literally true — a starter is a culture of wild yeast and lactobacillus bacteria living in a glass jar on your counter. You feed it, it ferments your flour into bread. There is no shortcut, but the work is mostly waiting.

## What you need

- A glass jar, about 500 ml capacity, with a loose-fitting lid
- A digital kitchen scale (do not try to measure flour by volume for this — it will not work)
- Strong white bread flour
- Whole rye flour (you only need a little — a small bag lasts months)
- Filtered or bottled water (chlorinated tap water can inhibit the culture)

## The two-week plan

### Day 1

Mix **50 g rye flour** + **50 g water** in the jar. Stir. Loose lid. Leave at room temperature (ideally 22–24°C / 72–75°F). Label the jar with the date. Forget about it for 24 hours.

### Days 2–3

You'll see small bubbles starting. Don't get excited — that's not yeast yet, it's bacteria. Discard half the mixture (compost it or use it in pancakes). Add **50 g strong white flour** + **50 g water**. Stir, replace lid, wait.

### Days 4–7

Repeat the discard + feed cycle once a day, same time of day. The mixture should start smelling sour, almost vinegary. It will rise after each feed, then fall.

### Days 8–10

By now, the starter should at least double in volume within 4–8 hours of being fed. The smell should be more bread-yeast than vinegar. If it's not doubling reliably, give it another 2–3 days of single feedings.

### Days 11–14

Switch to twice-daily feedings (morning and evening). This builds strength. The starter is ready to bake with when it consistently doubles in 4 hours after feeding, smells pleasantly sour, and floats when a small spoonful is dropped in water.

## Maintenance

A mature starter needs feeding once a day if it lives on the counter, or once a week if it lives in the fridge. The fridge version takes an extra feed or two to wake up before baking — plan ahead.

## What can go wrong

- **Pink, orange, or fuzzy mold:** Throw it out and start over. Healthy starters never grow visible mold.
- **A grey liquid on top ("hooch"):** The starter is hungry. Stir it back in, give it a feed.
- **No activity at all after 10 days:** Check the room temperature. Below 20°C / 68°F slows fermentation dramatically. Move the jar to a warmer spot.

## Your first loaf

Once the starter passes the float test, you can use it. There are countless beginner bread recipes — I'd suggest starting with a simple country loaf at 75% hydration, no shaping tricks yet. Get the routine first, then improve the bread.

Two weeks of feeding sounds like a commitment. It is. But once you have a healthy starter, you have it for years. Mine is six years old, has moved with me twice, and still bakes the same bread every weekend.
