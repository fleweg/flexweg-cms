Most lunchtime salads taste like a punishment. There's a reason — they're built around one wet leaf and three sad tomatoes, and they're missing every flavor and texture that makes food worth eating. Here's how to fix that.

A salad worth making has five things. Skip any of them and you end up with a sad bowl. Include all five and you have lunch.

## The five components

**1. Sturdy greens or grains as the base.** Spinach, kale (massaged with a little oil and lemon first), rocket, or — even better — cooked grains like farro, quinoa, or freekeh. The base needs to hold up against the dressing without wilting.

**2. Something substantial.** Roasted vegetables, beans, eggs, leftover chicken, tinned tuna or sardines, halloumi. This is what makes it a meal instead of a side. Don't skip it.

**3. Something crunchy.** Toasted seeds (pumpkin, sunflower), nuts, croutons, fried capers, raw fennel or radish. Texture is what makes you want a second bite.

**4. Something bright.** Pickled red onion (sliced thin, soaked in red wine vinegar for 10 minutes — game-changer). Lemon zest. Pomegranate seeds. A handful of fresh herbs. Sliced citrus. Without this, the salad falls flat halfway through.

**5. A real dressing.** Not bottled. Three parts olive oil to one part acid (lemon, sherry vinegar, red wine vinegar), salt, a teaspoon of Dijon mustard to emulsify. Whisk in a jar.

## An example, built from those five

- **Base:** Warm farro (cook once, eat for three days)
- **Substantial:** Roasted sweet potato cubes + a soft-boiled egg
- **Crunchy:** Toasted pumpkin seeds
- **Bright:** Pickled red onion + fresh dill + lemon zest
- **Dressing:** Olive oil + sherry vinegar + Dijon + salt

Put base in the bowl, top with everything else, dress just before eating. Twenty minutes of prep produces three lunches.

## The pickled red onion trick

This is the only "fancy" technique here, and it's not fancy. Slice half a red onion as thin as you can. Cover with red wine vinegar in a small jar. Add a pinch of salt and a pinch of sugar. Leave on the counter for 10 minutes minimum, or refrigerate for up to two weeks.

The onion turns bright pink, loses its harshness, gains a tangy bite. Use it on salads, tacos, sandwiches, eggs, anything that needs a lift.

## What to keep in the pantry

- Two or three good vinegars (sherry, red wine, apple cider)
- A jar of capers
- Tinned beans (chickpeas, white beans)
- A tin of good tuna or sardines
- Lemons (always — at least three in the bowl)
- Mixed seeds

With those + whatever fresh thing is in the fridge, you can build a real meal in 15 minutes any day of the week.

## A note on dressings

Make a small jar of dressing once a week. Three tablespoons of oil, one of vinegar, a teaspoon of Dijon, a pinch of salt. Shake. Lasts a week in the fridge. Cuts every weekday salad assembly time in half.

No more sad salads.
