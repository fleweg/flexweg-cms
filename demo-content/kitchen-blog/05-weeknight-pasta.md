Most weeknights I get home at seven, hungry, and not in the mood to think. This pasta is what I make. It's an iteration on Roman *pasta al pomodoro* — basically tomato, garlic, oil, pasta, parmesan. Twenty minutes from walking in the door to sitting down to eat.

## Ingredients (serves 2 generously)

- 200 g good dried pasta (penne, rigatoni, or spaghetti — your choice)
- 400 g good tinned tomatoes (San Marzano if your shop carries them)
- 4 cloves garlic, sliced thin
- 3 tbsp extra virgin olive oil
- A pinch of red pepper flakes
- Sea salt, black pepper
- Fresh basil if you have it, parsley if you don't
- Parmesan or pecorino, grated, for serving

## Method

**1. Get the water boiling.** Big pot, lots of water. When it boils, salt it heavily — "like the sea" is the cliché and it's accurate. Roughly one tablespoon per litre.

**2. While the water heats, start the sauce.** Cold pan, olive oil, sliced garlic, red pepper flakes. Set the heat to medium-low. The goal is to coax the garlic to soft and just barely golden, not to brown it. If it's browning, the heat is too high. About 4 minutes.

**3. Add the tomatoes.** Crush them with the back of a wooden spoon as you tip the tin in. Add a splash of water (use the empty tomato tin — rinses out the last of the juice). Season with salt. Simmer for 8–10 minutes while the pasta cooks.

**4. Pasta into the boiling water.** Cook to one minute less than the package suggests. The pasta finishes cooking in the sauce.

**5. Reserve a cup of pasta water.** Before you drain. This is the secret — the starchy water binds the sauce to the pasta.

**6. Combine.** Tip the pasta into the sauce. Stir vigorously for a minute, adding splashes of pasta water as needed until the sauce coats every piece. Off the heat, finish with torn basil and a generous handful of grated cheese.

**7. Serve.** More cheese, a final drizzle of olive oil, black pepper. Eat immediately.

## What's worth investing in

Three ingredients in this dish: the oil, the tomatoes, and the cheese. Spend more than you think you should on each. The difference between a €4 bottle of olive oil and a €12 one is bigger than between a €15 bottle of wine and a €40 one, and you're going to taste it directly. Real parmesan (Parmigiano Reggiano, with the rind stamped) lasts forever in the fridge and elevates any dish it touches.

The pasta itself? Get a decent brand — the bronze-die ones with the rough surface that holds sauce. But you don't need the most expensive.

## Variations

- Add a tin of anchovies (melted into the oil with the garlic) for a *puttanesca*-leaning version
- Swap basil for fresh chili and a squeeze of lemon at the end
- Stir a spoonful of cream cheese or ricotta in at the very end for richness

That's it. Real food, fast.
